GLOSSARY

Every Mahjong Word You'll Hear at the Table.

Every word that's specific to American Mah Jongg, organized by where you'll hear it. Skim it before your first game night. Bring it to the table if you want. Nobody minds.

You watched All's Fair in Love & Mahjong on Hallmark. The actresses said things in a confident clip and nobody paused to explain. So here it is. Every word that's specific to American Mah Jongg, organized by where you'll hear it.

A note on spelling. The National Mah Jongg League writes the name as "Mah Jongg." You will also see "Mahjong," "Mahjongg," and "Mah-Jongg" in the wild. They are all the same game. Prim prefers "Mahjong," the modern one-word form, for everyday use — but when she means the game's formal name, she writes it out in full: American Mah Jongg, the way the League does. Both are correct; society has long accepted both. The older spellings are a small homage to the women who wrote the rules down first, and the history they built.

Section One

The Tiles

A note on table talk. You will notice that the only time you hear a tile name spoken aloud is when someone is discarding it. Players do not announce what they are collecting, what they need, or what is in their hand. To do so would give the game away. This is part of the discipline of American Mah Jongg, and the source of much of its quiet drama.

Bam

Short for bamboo. One of the three suits, numbered 1 through 9, marked with bamboo stalks on the tile face. The 1 Bam is famously a bird, often a sparrow or a peacock.

You'll hear it as: "Four bam." Called by a player as she discards the tile face up. The number always comes before the suit.

Blank

An unmarked tile, included with most American Mah Jongg sets as a replacement piece. If a tile is ever lost from the set, the owner can mark a blank to stand in for it, sparing the cost of buying a whole new set. Blanks are also sometimes brought into social play as a bonus tile, used to recover a tile that was lost to the discard pile in a previous round. Whether to play with blanks is a matter of house rules. Many tables do not allow them. Others find them a kindness, especially in social or learning games. Blanks are not part of the 152-tile count of a standard playing set.

When blanks are played, the typical house rule is to add either two or four. They are added to the end of a player's wall, turning a normal wall of 19 stacks into a wall of 20. With two blanks, one player has the longer wall. With four, two players do. A blank can never be used to claim a Joker. The Joker exchange requires a real tile in hand.

Crak

Short for character. The second suit, numbered 1 through 9, marked with a number and a small Chinese character.

You'll hear it as: "Seven crak." Called only when the tile is being discarded.

Dot

The third suit. Numbered 1 through 9, marked with circles. Sometimes called Circles.

You'll hear it as: "Two dot." Called only when the tile is being discarded.

Dragon

Three families of tiles: the Green Dragon, the Red Dragon, and the White Dragon (more commonly called Soap at American tables). One Dragon family pairs with each numbered suit on the card. Four of each family in the set, twelve Dragons total.

Green Dragon

One of the three Dragon tiles. The green-marked Dragon, paired with the Bam suit on most NMJL cards. Four in a standard American set.

Red Dragon

One of the three Dragon tiles. The red-marked Dragon, paired with the Crak suit on most NMJL cards. Four in a standard American set.

White Dragon

The third of the three Dragon tiles, paired with the Dot suit on most NMJL cards. Called Soap at almost every American table. Printed as a stylized zero or sometimes left blank. Four in a standard American set.

Flower

A specially illustrated tile, often painted with flowers or seasons. Eight Flowers in an American set. They appear in specific hands on the card.

Honor tile

Umbrella term for any tile that is not part of the three numbered suits. The Winds and the Dragons are honor tiles.

Joker

The wild tile. A Joker can stand in for any tile inside a group of three or more identical tiles (a Pung, Kong, Quint, or Sextet). It cannot be used in a pair, in a single, or in a run (a group of different tiles, like a 1-2-3-4-5 sequence). The rule players say at the table: never in pairs, never in singles, never in runs. There are eight Jokers in an American set, which is one of the things that makes American Mah Jongg American.

You'll hear it as: "She just discarded a joker, and I needed that. Can't touch it now, it's dead." A discarded joker cannot be called by another player. The tile is lost to the round.

Soap

The American nickname for the White Dragon, which is printed as a stylized zero or sometimes left blank. Called Soap because it looks like a bar of soap on edge. On most NMJL cards, Soap functions as both the Dragon for the Dot suit and as a zero in year hands.

Suit

A family of numbered tiles, 1 through 9. American Mah Jongg has three suits: Bams, Craks, and Dots.

Tile

A single playing piece. American sets have 152 tiles total: 108 numbered tiles, 16 Winds, 12 Dragons, 8 Flowers, and 8 Jokers.

Wind

Four tiles labeled N, E, W, and S, for the four directions. Four of each in the set, sixteen Winds total. American tiles use Roman letters only.

You'll hear it as: "East." Called when discarding. The direction stands alone, no number.

Section Two

The Table and the Set

Bettor

A fifth person at a four-player table who sits out the hand and bets on which player will win. The Bettor wins or loses with the player she bet on. NMJL has formal rules for Bettor play.

Card, the

The annual hand list published by the National Mah Jongg League. It lists every winning combination of tiles you can build this year, organized into sections. The card expires when next year's card is published. Without the card, American Mah Jongg cannot be played.

You'll hear it as: "Did you get this year's card?"

East

The dealer's seat. The dealer is called East regardless of where she actually sits in your living room. The role rotates with each hand.

NMJL

National Mah Jongg League. The organization that publishes the original card and sets the rules for American Mah Jongg. Founded in New York in 1937. Alternative cards exist from third-party publishers, but the NMJL card remains the standard.

You'll hear it as: "It's an NMJL hand."

Pusher

The plastic arms (sometimes called pusher arms) used to slide each player's wall toward the center of the table. Most American Mah Jongg sets include four.

Rack

The long tray that holds your tiles facing you. Has a back ledge for tiles in your hand and a front ledge for tiles you have exposed.

Wall

The four lines of stacked tiles built at the start of each hand. Players draw from the wall in turn.

Section Three

The Charleston

Across

The player seated directly opposite you, and also the second tile pass in each Charleston (the pass that goes across the table).

Blind pass

A pass that includes one or two tiles you have not looked at, taken from the tiles just handed to you. Allowed only on the third pass of the First Charleston (Left) and the third pass of the Second Charleston (Right).

Charleston

The opening tile exchange. Before the game starts, every player passes three tiles to the right, three across, three to the left. After that, players vote on whether to do a Second Charleston: three left, three across, three right. Then a Courtesy pass between partners. The Charleston is how a hand goes from random to playable.

You'll hear it as: "Are we doing the Second Charleston?"

Courtesy pass

The final exchange of the Charleston, between players sitting across from each other. Up to three tiles are exchanged, by mutual agreement between the two players involved. Only you and your across partner need to agree on the number. The other across pair at the table works independently and may settle on a different number, or pass none at all.

First Charleston

The first round of three passes. Right, then Across, then Left. The Left pass may include blind tiles.

Second Charleston

The optional second round, performed only if all four players agree. Left, then Across, then Right. The Right pass may include blind tiles.

Section Four

Playing the Hand

Call

To claim a tile that another player just discarded, in order to complete a Pung, Kong, Quint, or Sextet, or to declare Mah Jongg. You must call before the next player draws.

Concealed

Hidden in your rack. A concealed group is one that has not been exposed.

Discard

A tile you place face up in the center of the table after your turn. Discards are gone from your rack. They become available for the next player to call, or to no one if no one calls.

Expose

To lay down tiles face up on your rack, so the table can see them. You expose when you call a tile.

Exposed

Tiles that have been laid face up on your rack. Once exposed, they cannot be moved back into your hand.

Joker exchange

A swap. If a player has an exposed Joker on her rack, any other player, on her own turn, who holds the actual tile the Joker stands in for may request to swap the real tile for the Joker. You do not reach across the table. You do not touch another player's tiles. You ask. The player holding the Joker cannot refuse the swap. It is a bit of a formality and a dance, and part of the ceremony. Sometimes called Joker redemption.

Kong

Four of a kind. Four identical tiles, used in hands on the card that call for a Kong.

Pair

Two of a kind. The smallest group on the card. Pairs cannot use Jokers.

Pivot

To abandon the hand you're building and switch to a different one mid-game, usually because the tiles you've drawn or the tiles you've seen discarded make a different hand more reachable. Pivoting early in a hand is often smart. Pivoting late is risky because you've already given up tiles you can't get back.

You'll hear it as: "I had to pivot when she exposed that pung."

Pung

Three of a kind. The most common group on the card.

You'll hear it as: "Pung," said when a player calls a discarded tile to complete three of a kind.

Quint

Five of a kind. Quint hands do not appear on the card every year. They come and go from one year's edition to the next, depending on what the League chose to publish.

Self-pick

Winning by drawing your final tile from the wall on your own turn, rather than by calling someone else's discard. Sometimes called self-draw. The distinction matters most when the table is keeping score, since self-picks are typically rewarded with extra points.

Sextet

Six of a kind. Rare, but real. Appears on certain hands on the card.

Single

One tile. Singles cannot use Jokers.

Section Five

On the Card

The card is organized into sections, each with a theme. You don't need to memorize the sections to play, but you will hear them named.

2468

A section of the card built from even-numbered tiles (2, 4, 6, 8).

13579

A section built from odd-numbered tiles.

369

A section built from threes, sixes, and nines.

Consecutive Run

A section using sequential numbers (for example, 1-2-3-4-5).

Like Numbers

A section using the same number across multiple suits (for example, all the 5s).

Quints

The section of the card that calls for hands containing five-of-a-kind groups.

Singles and Pairs

A section where every group is either a single tile or a pair. No Pungs, Kongs, or Jokers. This section is known for its higher point values and concealed hands.

Year hand

A hand built around the digits of the current year. The 2026 card has a year section using 2, 0, 2, 6.

Section Six

Winning and Ending the Hand

Concealed hand

A hand played all the way to Mah Jongg without ever exposing a tile. Most hands on the card pay double for concealed. Some hands on the card are designated concealed, marked at the end of the line with a C followed by the point value (for example, C – 50).

Dead hand

A hand that has been declared illegal or unwinnable, usually because of an exposure error or an incorrect call. A dead hand sits out the rest of the round. If you know your hand is dead, you do not have to claim it as such. You may begin full defensive play to prevent others from winning.

Earthly Hand

A Mah Jongg declared by a non-dealer on her own first draw from the wall, before any tile has been called or discarded. Extremely rare. Many house rules pay double for an Earthly Hand. Not an NMJL-official bonus.

Heavenly Hand

A Mah Jongg declared by the dealer (East) using the original 14 tiles she was dealt, before any other play has happened. Rarer still. Talked about more than seen. Many house rules pay double or triple. Not an NMJL-official bonus.

Looking

Said by a player who is one tile away from winning. Used to alert the table to play defensively.

You'll hear it as: "I'm looking."

Mah Jongg

The winning call. When your tiles match a hand on the card, you say "Mah Jongg" and the hand ends. Pronounce it however the players at your table do.

You'll hear it as: "Mah Jongg!" Followed by quiet. Followed by everyone counting.

No Jokers

A hand played without using any Jokers. Many house rules pay a small bonus for a no-Joker win. Not an NMJL rule, but widely played.

Wall game

When the wall runs out before anyone calls Mah Jongg. No one wins. Sometimes called a draw.

Section Seven

What You'll Hear at the Table

Cold tile

A tile that no one seems to want, safe to discard. The opposite of hot.

Hot tile

A tile that someone almost certainly needs, dangerous to discard. Some teachers also use "hot wall" informally for the last few rows of the wall, when discards become risky.

Stop!

Called by a player who wants to claim a discard before the next player draws. The next player must wait. If the call holds, play continues from the caller. If not, play resumes.
