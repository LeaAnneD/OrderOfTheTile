## PAGE 1 — A Letter from Prim

> *Eyebrow, set small, all caps, Lilac:*  THE 2026 COMPANION · LETTER ONE

> *Headline, Playfair Display SC, large:*  On the 2026 Season

*Body, Crimson Text:*

The card has changed and you can feel it. Last year asked one kind of patience from you. This year asks another.

There are sixty-nine printed hands and one thousand four hundred fifty-eight permutations under them — a much wider field than 2025 gave you. Quints have come back to their own section after a year away. The flower content is heavy, heavier than the years immediately behind it, which means your pulls and your passes will have to account for petals you cannot afford to discard carelessly. The structure leans pung-and-kong, and the year digits are scattered across all three suits, which is the card's quiet way of telling you to keep your options open longer than you are used to.

I have spent the season with this card the way I spend every season — slowly, with a cup of something warm, and a pencil. What I have written in the pages that follow is what I would tell you across the table if we had the time. It is not a cheat sheet. There are plenty of those, and most of them are honest enough about what they are. This is something else.

The pages are arranged the way I think about a hand. First the map, because nothing else makes sense without it. Then what to hold and what to release, because most of your decisions live there. Then the ten sections, briefly, because you already know how to read a card and I will not insult you by re-printing it. Then the Quints, who deserve their own page after the year they had. And then the discipline pages — flowers, jokers, the Charleston — because that is where this card will be won or lost.

A note before you turn the page. I do not believe in general advice. *General advice is not advice.* Every line in this Companion is meant to be specific enough that you could disagree with it and we could have an actual conversation about why. If you find yourself nodding along without friction, read it again. I have probably said something you ought to push back on.

The table is set. Let us begin.

> *Signature line, Playfair italic, Orchid:*  Prim

## PAGE 2 — The Map

> *Eyebrow:*  THE 2026 COMPANION · LETTER TWO

> *Headline:*  The Map. Read this first.

*Lead paragraph, Crimson Text:*

The card looks like ten sections. It is really six pivot paths. A pivot is what you do when the hand you started building is no longer the hand most likely to win, and the rack you are holding is already most of the way to a different one. The card rewards players who recognize their pivots before they need them.

*Layout instruction for InDesign:*
> Center of page: a pivot diagram drawn in Lilac line-work over a Cloud background, with the six paths labeled in Orchid. Use the seal / wax aesthetic from `images/seal.png`. The diagram is the page. Body copy wraps left and right of it as a thin column on each side, or sits beneath it as a single block.

*The six paths, set as short titled paragraphs in two columns beneath the diagram:*

**The interior pivot — within 2026.**  The 2026 section is the most generous part of the card this year. The hands inside it share more tiles with each other than they share with anything outside it. If your rack is already deep in a 2026 hand, do not abandon it for a glamour move. Pivot within the section first.

**The number-anchor pivot — 2468 ↔ 13579.**  These two sections share the rhythm of even versus odd, but the *anchor tiles* — the tiles you commit to early — overlap less than players assume. The pivot here is real but it is narrow. You will know it is available when your singles tell you, not when the hand list does.

**The like-numbers pivot — Any Like Numbers ↔ Consecutive Run.**  This is the most forgiving pivot on the card. A rack heavy in matched numbers across suits is rarely far from a workable run. Watch your dragons; they are the deciding factor on whether the pivot is clean.

**The Quints pivot.**  Quints hands almost always pivot to other Quints hands or out of the card entirely. Treat the Quints section as an island. If you commit, commit. If you abandon, abandon early.

**The wind & dragon pivot — into Singles & Pairs.**  This is the pivot that surprises people. A rack that is failing in Winds & Dragons is often closer to Singles & Pairs than the player realizes, because both sections reward held singletons rather than developed pungs. If your winds are not running, look at this path before you panic.

**The 369 pivot — into Consecutive Run.**  3, 6, and 9 share suits with the most common run patterns. A 369 hand that has slowed is often a Consecutive Run hand that has not yet noticed itself.

*Page-bottom callout, set in Butter against Ink, single line:*
> THE MAP IS NOT THE CARD. THE CARD IS WHAT YOU HAVE IN YOUR RACK RIGHT NOW.

## PAGE 3 — Hold, Pass, Pivot

> *Eyebrow:*  THE 2026 COMPANION · LETTER THREE

> *Headline:*  What to hold. What to release. When to turn.

*Lead, Crimson Text:*

I will be specific now. The next three columns are what I would do at the start of a hand on this card, and what I would say to you if you were sitting beside me deciding what to keep.

*Three-column layout in InDesign, equal width, set with hairline rules between in Lilac.*

### HOLD.

**The 6.**  More hands need this tile than any other number on the card. It appears across eight of the ten sections. Hold your sixes longer than instinct tells you to.

**Flowers, until the third Charleston pass.**  This card was built around its flowers. They are not optional decoration this year. If you are flower-shy by habit, change the habit for this card.

**The 3.**  Second only to the 6 for cross-section utility. Strong in 369, in 13579, in Consecutive Run.

**Pairs that point in two directions.**  A pair of the same tile across suits, or a 2 and a 6 in the same suit, is worth more than its weight on this card because it keeps you live across multiple sections at once.

**Jokers, longer than you want to.**  See page six.

### PASS.

**The 1, by default.**  The 1 is the narrowest number tile on this card. It is critical to a small handful of hands, and if you are not on one of them, you are passing 1s and not regretting it.

**Most lone winds.**  Winds without dragons, and dragons without winds, are passing tiles unless you are already committed to a Winds & Dragons hand or the Singles & Pairs pivot. Do not collect them out of habit.

**Lone 9s, lone 8s.**  The 8 is moderately useful; the 9 is narrow. Neither one is a tile to defend without context.

**Singletons that touch nothing.**  If a tile in your rack is not within one move of a pair, a pung, or a planned set, it is paying rent it cannot afford.

### PIVOT.

**Pivot when your strongest pair is not in your strongest section.**  The single most reliable signal that your hand has changed underneath you. Trust the pair, not the plan.

**Pivot before you see a joker.**  Players wait for a joker as if it is permission. Pivots made before a joker arrives are cheaper than pivots made after.

**Pivot once. Twice if forced. Never three times.**  A hand that has pivoted three times is not a pivoting hand. It is a confused one. Recognize this and play defensively for the rest of the round.

*Page-bottom callout, Orchid:*
> *Most of strategy is keeping the right tile one turn longer than feels comfortable. The 6 is that tile this year.*

## PAGE 4 — The Ten Sections, In Brief

> *Eyebrow:*  THE 2026 COMPANION · LETTER FOUR

> *Headline:*  The ten sections, briefly.

*Lead:*

You have the card. I will not reprint it. What follows is what I would whisper to you about each section as you considered committing to it. One paragraph each. Read them in any order. The strategic note at the end of each is what I would actually want you to remember.

*Set as ten short blocks in a 2-column grid, each block titled in Playfair small caps, Orchid:*

**2026.**  The year section is generous this year. It pivots inside itself better than any other section on the card, and the digits are split across suits, which sounds inconvenient and is actually a gift — your suit choice can be made late.  *Watch for:* The interior pivot. Do not leave the section for a flashier hand.

**2468.**  Even-number territory. Strong on 6s, weaker on 2s and 8s than you might expect. The hands here ask for kongs more than the card's average.  *Watch for:* Joker pressure. Even-number hands eat jokers.

**13579.**  Odd-number territory. Steadier than 2468 and slightly more forgiving on its anchors.  *Watch for:* The 5 and the 7. They are quieter than the 3, but they appear together more often than the card's first read suggests.

**369.**  A small section with a long reach, because 3 and 6 are both broadly useful tiles. The 369 hand that fails has usually failed at the 9.  *Watch for:* The pivot to Consecutive Run.

**Any Like Numbers.**  The most generous section for a rack already in good shape. It rewards parallel thinking — same number across suits — and punishes specialization.  *Watch for:* Don't get lured here when your rack is fragmented. ALN looks easy and is not.

**Consecutive Run.**  Predictable in shape, less predictable in suit. The runs ask for clean dragons and rarely forgive flower scarcity.  *Watch for:* Your dragons. They decide this section.

**Quints.**  An island. See page five.

**Winds & Dragons.**  Ceremonial and unforgiving. A hand committed to W&D is committed; the pivot out goes to Singles & Pairs, not back to the number sections.  *Watch for:* Your wind balance early. If you have three winds and no fourth in two passes of the Charleston, you do not have a W&D hand.

**Singles & Pairs.**  The most defensive section, and an underrated pivot landing zone. It rewards a rack of held singletons and small pairs, which is what most failing hands look like in act two.  *Watch for:* Your jokers — most S&P hands restrict them. Plan accordingly.

## PAGE 5 — The Return of the Quints

> *Eyebrow:*  THE 2026 COMPANION · LETTER FIVE

> *Headline:*  The Quints came back. Here is what you should know.

*Lead:*

In 2024 the Quints section produced one of the strangest hands in recent NMJL memory — a single Quints/2 hand that, by itself, accounted for more than eight hundred permutations. In 2025 the League removed Quints as a standalone section entirely; quint-style hands were embedded as variants inside other groups, and a year passed in which the Quints player was not really a player at all. In 2026 they returned.

This is the page of the Companion that is most uniquely mine, because The Order of the Tile has spent the better part of a year archiving the modern history of this card *(see prim.orderofthetile.com/nmjl-card-history.html)*. So I will tell you what the archive tells me, and then I will tell you what I think.

*Subhead, Playfair small caps:*  WHAT THE ARCHIVE SHOWS

A hand of five identical tiles costs more jokers than any other hand structure. Quints sections have historically asked the rack for a kind of all-or-nothing commitment that does not pivot well. The 2024 monster Quints/2 hand was generous because the permutation count was so absurdly high; the rack could find its way home from many starting points. **The 2026 Quints section is not that generous.** It is a normal section, with a normal number of hands, and normal joker economics — which is to say, demanding ones.

*Subhead:*  WHAT I THINK

Commit early or do not commit. The Quints player who hesitates loses. If your rack at the second Charleston is showing four of a kind in a useful suit, you may have a Quints hand. If it is showing three of a kind plus a wandering single, you do not. Pass the single and reconsider.

The pivot out of Quints is almost never to another section. It is out of the card — which is to say, to a defensive game. This is honest, not dire. Knowing that Quints does not pivot is what allows you to commit to it without anxiety.

If the Charleston gives you nothing useful in three passes, *do not chase Quints out of nostalgia for last year's section coming back*. Sentiment is not strategy.

*Pull quote, Orchid italic, large:*
> *A Quints hand is not a flexible hand. That is its honesty. Treat it as such, and it will play you straight.*

## PAGE 6 — Flowers, Jokers, and the Charleston

> *Eyebrow:*  THE 2026 COMPANION · LETTER SIX

> *Headline:*  Three disciplines for the 2026 card.

*Lead:*

Strategy, on most cards, is a matter of choosing the right hand. On this card, three running disciplines will cost you more games than your hand selection will. Read these in order. Re-read them after a session in which you lost a game you thought you had.

*Three-section layout, stacked vertically, each with a numbered marker in Melon:*

### 01  ·  Flower discipline.

The 2026 card is flower-heavy. *Heavy* is not a marketing word here; it is a structural fact. More hands than usual want flowers, and several hands want more flowers than usual.

What this means at the table:
- Do not pass flowers in the first Charleston unless you have a clear non-flower hand committed.
- Do not discard flowers in the early game. The cost of a flower retrieved from the discard pile is a joker, and you cannot afford that math on this card.
- A flower in your rack at the start of act two is a real asset, not a curiosity. Build around it.

### 02  ·  Joker economics.

Quints and Singles & Pairs restrict jokers more than the rest of the card by NMJL rule, and several specific hands across other sections add their own restrictions. *Knowing which of your candidate hands restricts jokers is half of joker strategy.*

What this means at the table:
- Hold jokers longer than the player on your left expects you to.
- Do not play a joker into a kong you could complete naturally within two pulls.
- Joker passes — when they happen at the table — are seen. Do not signal a hand you have not committed to by accepting one.

### 03  ·  The Charleston, on this card.

The first Charleston is for orientation. Do not over-commit. The second Charleston is for selection — by the end of it, you should know your hand, your section, and your color. The third Charleston is for *the pivot you didn't expect*. If your second-pass rack has not narrowed your hand to one or two candidates, the third pass is not for finishing your plan; it is for picking up the pivot you didn't see coming.

Six pivot paths sit on this card (page two). Two of them are most likely to surface in the third Charleston: the **Winds & Dragons → Singles & Pairs** pivot, when your winds did not arrive, and the **369 → Consecutive Run** pivot, when your 9s did not. Pass and accept accordingly.
